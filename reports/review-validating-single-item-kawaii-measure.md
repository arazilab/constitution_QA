# Review, Validating the Single Item Kawaii Measure

This paper studies whether a one item self report measure of kawaii can be used as a valid measure in HCI and CUI research. The authors combine nine datasets with 967 unique participants in Japan, covering voice assistants, video game character voices, visual character bodies, and mixed voice plus body stimuli. The paper is timely because kawaii is becoming a relevant design and user experience concept for conversational agents, game characters, and voice interfaces. The topic fits well with ACM CUI, and the paper gives useful early evidence for a measure that the field is already using.

The main strength is that the paper addresses a clear measurement gap. It does not just assume that the single item kawaii rating works. It tries to test that assumption using several forms of validity evidence, including convergent validity, known groups evidence, and cross context evidence. The use of multiple datasets and modalities is also a strength. The results suggest that the item is meaningfully related to nearby concepts such as happiness, favourability, humanlikeness, and low artificiality. This gives some practical confidence that the item is not random or empty.

The main weakness is that the statistical evidence may be stronger on paper than it really is. The sample sizes in the results seem to count many participant by stimulus ratings, not only unique participants. If repeated ratings from the same people or the same stimuli are treated as fully independent, the p values can look too strong. The paper should either account for this nesting or make the claims more cautious. A second important weakness is the reliability claim. Cronbach alpha is reported for related multi item sets, but that does not show reliability for the single kawaii item itself. The authors should soften this claim unless they can add test retest evidence or another suitable reliability check.

My recommendation is Weak Accept. The contribution is moderate, but useful. The paper should be clearer that this is initial validity evidence, not a full validation of the measure. I would suggest focusing the revision on two changes. First, explain the unit of analysis and how repeated ratings were handled. Second, soften the claims about reliability and cross modality validity. With those changes, this would be a clear and useful poster for the CUI community.
